Compound Interest I

Now we can dive into compound interest. Similar to simple interest, there is also a formula to calculate compound interest. But instead of telling directly what it is, we want to invite you to ‘invent’ the formula by yourself! Don’t worry, we believe you can do it if you read through the instructions and prompts. Enjoy the invention!

Definition

Understanding the text definition is the first step to inventing your formula. A very simple way to comprehend this concept is: compound interest is the interest you earn on interest. When you were calculating simple interest, the interest was generated out of a constant amount of principal. But here as you can see from the definition, the interest you earned in the previous year becomes part of the ‘principal’ of the next year.

Invent the formula

Let’s use an example to help you approach the formula of compound interest. (Note: the figures in the examples are all imaginary and do not represent the practical cases.)


Example

Your friend Jasmine is planning to save her allowance in the bank because she wants to visit New York in the future using her own money. Her current deposit is $1000, and she learns that the annual interest rate the bank offers is 5%. Now she wants you to help her evaluate whether she will gain enough money for the trip.

1. Suppose she starts saving money at the beginning of this year. How much will she earn by the end of the year?

Hint

2. How much will she earn by the end of the second year? Remember: based on the definition of compound interest, your current principal should involve the interest from the first year.


3. How much will she earn by the end of the third year?



Let’s Pause for a second.

Take a look at the three problems you’ve just solved. Is your calculation process similar to the method below?

1st Year: 1000 x (1+ 5%) = 1050
2nd Year: 1050 x (1+5%) = 1102.5
3rd Year: 1102.5 x (1+5%) = 1157.625


Let’s Think.

How much will she earn by the end of the 10th year? What about the 30th year? Are you still willing to calculate it year by year? I guess not. So this is why we need a formula—to simplify the process of repetitive tasks and approach the result faster!


Look at your calculation process on the paper again. If we want to generate a widely applied pattern for these three expressions, you may need to…




Perform these methods by yourself. Have you found any patterns? Are you able to figure out the final amount at the end of the 10th year?

1st Year: 1000 x (1+ 5%) = 1050
2nd Year: 1050 x (1+5%) = 1102.5
3rd Year: 1102.5 x (1+5%) = 1157.625

1000 x (1+ 5%) = 1050
1000 x 1050 x (1+5%)^2 = 1050 x 1102.5
1000 x 1050 x 1102.5 x (1+5%)^3 = 1050 x 1102.5 x 1157.625
